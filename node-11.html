3.3 Frequency response and filtering

3.3.1 Frequency response of an LTI system

Impulse response and frequency response

  • â¢ Most LTI systems can be expressed as a convolution of the form:

    \[y(t)=x(t)\star h(t)\]

    where \(h(t)\) is called the impulse response (the response of the system to an input \(x(t)=\delta (t)\))

  • â¢ The Fourier transform of the LTI system relation is

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {11} \)

    \begin{equation} \label {eq:rep_freq_syst} Y(f)=H(f)X(f) \end{equation}

  • â¢ The frequency response \(H(f)\) (also called transfer function) of the LTI system is the Fourier transform of \(h(t)\):

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {12} \)

    \begin{equation} \label {eq:rep_freq_syst2} H(f)=\frac {Y(f)}{X(f)} \end{equation}

Response to a mono-frequency signal

  • â¢ For a system of impulse response \(h(t)\) with an input \(x(t)=e^{2j\pi f_0 t}\)

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {13} \)

    \begin{align*} y(t) &= \int _{-\infty }^{+\infty }h(\tau )e^{2j\pi f_0 h(t-\tau )}d\tau \\ &= e^{2j\pi f_0 t} \int _{-\infty }^{+\infty }h(\tau )e^{- 2 j\pi f_0 h\tau }d\tau \\ &=e^{2j\pi f_0 t} H(f_0)=x(t)H(f_0) \end{align*}

  • â¢ An input signal with unique frequency \(f_0\) is multiplied by \(H(f_0)\).

  • â¢ Its amplitude is multiplied by \(|H(f_0)|\) and a phase \(Arg(H(f_0))\) is added.

  • â¢ The complex exponential is an eigenvector of the convolution operator.

Static gain The complex static gain is the constant \(K\) such that

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {13} \)

\begin{equation*} K=H(0)=\int _{-\infty }^{+\infty }h(t)dt \end{equation*}

Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) The system is defined by an equation of the form:

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {13} \)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:edo2} a_0y(t)+a_1\frac {dy(t)}{dt}+\dots +a_n\frac {d^ny(t)}{dt^n}= b_0x(t)+b_1\frac {dx(t)}{dt} +\dots +b_m\frac {d^mx(t)}{dt^m} \end{equation}

Frequency response of an ODE

  • â¢ We recall the properties of the FT for th n-th derivative of a function:

    \[ \mathcal {F}\left [\frac {d^{(n)}x(t)}{dt^n}\right ]=(2i\pi f)^nX(f)=(iw)^nX(w)\]

  • â¢ The Frequency response of the ODE can be expressed as

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {14} \)

    \begin{equation} \label {eq:edo_transf} H(w)=\frac {Y(w)}{X(w)}=\frac {b_0+b_1jw+\dots +b_m(jw)^m}{a_0+a_1jw+\dots +a_n(jw)^n} \end{equation}

3.3.2 Representation of the frequency response

Frequency interpretation of the frequency response

  • â¢ The frequency response of a system gives information on the transformations due to the system.

  • â¢ Quantities that can be plotted :

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {15} \)

    \begin{align*} \tilde H(w)&= Re(\tilde H(w))+jIm(\tilde H(w))\\ &=|\tilde H(w)|e^{jArg(\tilde H(w))} \end{align*}

    • â \(|\tilde H(w)|\) modulus of the frequency response.

    • â \(Arg(\tilde H(w))=\angle \tilde H(w)=\tan ^{-1}\left (\frac {Im(\tilde H(w)}{Re(\tilde H(w)}\right )\) phase in radian.

Graphical representation of systems

  • â¢ Bode plot (Modulus+Argument).

  • â¢ Nichols/Black plot (Modulus VS Argument).

  • â¢ Nyquist plot (Real VS Imaginary)

Bode plot

Definition The Bode plot of a system is composed of two plots that are function of \(w\):

  • â¢ Magnitude (or gain) in decibels (dB)

    \[\tilde G(w)=20\log _{10}{(|\tilde H(w)|)}\]

  • â¢ Phase in degrees or radians

    \[\tilde \Phi (w)=Arg(H(w)|)=\angle |\tilde H(w)|\]

The scale of the radial frequency \(w\) is logarithmic, which means that for a rational frequency response \(H\) one will be mostly piecewise linear.

Properties of the Bode plot The logarithm and the argument allows for simple diagrams for combination of systems

Multiplication If two LTIs \(\tilde H_1(w)\) and \(\tilde H_2(w)\) are in series the the equivalent system is \(\tilde H(w)=\tilde H_1(w)\tilde H_2(w)\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde G(w)=\tilde G_1(w)+\tilde G_2(w)\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde \Phi (w)=\tilde \Phi _1(w)+\tilde \Phi _2(w)\)

Division If and LTI can be expressed as \(\displaystyle \tilde H(w)=\frac {\tilde H_1(w)}{\tilde H_2(w)}\) then

  • â¢ \(\tilde G(w)=\tilde G_1(w)-\tilde G_2(w)\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde \Phi (w)=\tilde \Phi _1(w)-\tilde \Phi _2(w)\)

This is particularly useful for rational frequency responses such as ODE.

Nichosl plot The Nichols plot (Diagramme de Black in France) is a parametric plot of \(\tilde H(w)\) with \(20\log _{10}|\tilde H(w)|\) on y-axis and phase \(\tilde \Phi (w)\) on x-axis.

  • â¢ Show theM odulus/Phase trajectory as a function of \(w\).

  • â¢ Can be ploted following the Bode plot \(w\).

Nyquist plot

Definition The Nyquist plot is a parametric plot of \(\tilde H(w)\) with \(Real(\tilde H(w))\) on x-axis and \(Imag(\tilde \Phi (w))\) on y-axis.

  • â¢ Show the trajectory of \(\tilde H\) in the complex plane.

  • â¢ Used in system control to study the stability of systems.

Frequency response of electronic systems

Principle Ohmâs law can be extended to capacitors and inductors using what is called complex called electrical impedance. The linear system \(i(t)\rightarrow u(t)\) is expressed as

\[\tilde U(w)=\tilde H(w)\tilde I(w)=\tilde Z(w)\tilde I(w)\]

Resistor

  • â¢ \(u(t)=Ri(t)\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde U(w)=R\tilde I(w)\)

  • â¢ \(Z_R=R\)

Capacitor

  • â¢ \(u(t)=\frac {1}{C}\int _{-\infty }^ti(u)du\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde U(w)=\frac {1}{jCw}\tilde I(w)\)

  • â¢ \(Z_C=\frac {1}{jCw}\)

Inductor

  • â¢ \(u(t)=L\frac {di(t)}{dt}\)

  • â¢ \(\tilde U(w)=jLw\tilde I(w)\)

  • â¢ \(Z_L=jLw\)

The frequency response of passive electronic systems can be computed with simple computation of complex numbers.

    \[ \tilde H(w)=\frac {\tilde Y(w)}{\tilde X(w)}=\pause \frac {Z_C}{Z_L+Z_R+Z_C}=\frac {\frac {1}{jCw}}{\frac {1}{jCw}+R+jLw}\]

  • â¢ Normalized frequency response

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {16} \)

    \begin{equation*} \tilde H(w)=\frac {1}{1+RCjw+LC(jw)^2}=\frac {k}{1+2z\frac {jw}{w_n}+(\frac {jw}{w_n})^2} \end{equation*}

    • â \(k\) Static gain : \(k=\pause {1}\)

    • â \(z\) damping ratio of the system : \(z=\pause {\frac {R}{2}\sqrt {\frac {C}{L}}}\)

    • â \(w_n\) natural frequency of the system : \(w_n=\pause {\frac {1}{\sqrt {LC}}}\)

Linear differential equation The second order differential equation corresponding to the system is

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {16} \)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:syst_second_ordre} \frac {d^2y(t)}{dt^2}+2zw_n \frac {dy(t)}{dt}+w_n^2y(t)=kw_n^2x(t) \end{equation}

Factorization The second order system can be factorized as

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {17} \)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:transfsecondordre3} \tilde H(w)=\frac {kw_n^2}{(jw-c_1)(jw-c_2)} \end{equation}

with

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {18} \)

\begin{align} \label {eq:6} c_1&={-zw_n+w_n\sqrt {z^2-1}}\\ c_2&={-zw_n-w_n\sqrt {z^2-1}} \end{align} \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are called the poles of the transfer function.

Response of the system for \(z>1\)

  • â¢ \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are real coefficients.

  • â¢ The FT can be expressed as

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {20} \)

    \begin{equation} \label {eq:transfsecondordre5} \tilde H(w)=\frac {M}{jw-c_1}-\frac {M}{jw-c_2} \end{equation}

    with \(M=\frac {w_n}{2\sqrt {z^2-1}}\),

  • â¢ The impulse response of the system is

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {21} \)

    \begin{equation*} h(t)=M(e^{c_1t}-e^{c_2t})\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

  • â¢ The step response of the system is

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {21} \)

    \begin{equation*} e(t)=\left (1+M\left (\frac {e^{c_1t}}{c_1}-\frac {e^{c_2t}}{c_2}\right )\right )\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

Response of the system for \(z=1\) The FT becomes:

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {21} \)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:transfsecondordre3} \tilde H(w)=\frac {kw_n^2}{(jw+w_n)^2} \end{equation}

that is the square of one first order system.

The impulse response fo the system can be expressed as

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {22} \)

\begin{equation*} h(t)=w_n^2te^{-w_nt}\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

The step response can be expressed as

\( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {22} \)

\begin{equation*} e(t)=(1-e^{-w_nt}-w_nte^{-w_nt})\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

Response of the system for \(z<1\)

  • â¢ In this case the damping is weak and oscillations appear.

  • â¢ This comes from teh fact that when \(z<1\) coefficients \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are complex. The impulse response is

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {22} \)

    \begin{equation*} h(t)=M(e^{c_1t}-e^{c_2t})\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

  • â¢ The step response is

    \( \seteqsection {3} \) \( \seteqnumber {22} \)

    \begin{equation*} h(t)=\frac {w_ne^{-zw_nt}}{\sqrt {1-z^2}}\sin \left (w_nt\sqrt {1-z^2}\right )\Gamma (t) \end{equation*}

    that is a sine with an exponentially decreasing magnitude.
